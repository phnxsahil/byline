# Phase 1: Full Market & Competitor Report

This report explains what Mkaan.AI is, who it serves, how it differs from other construction-tech players, and what market position it is trying to own.
The goal is to help you understand the product, the competitive set, and the moat in a way you can present to a founder.

## 1. What Mkaan.AI is

Mkaan.AI is an AI-powered copilot for first-time home builders in India.
Its main promise is to help ordinary homeowners understand construction costs, monitor progress, and access expert help without paying traditional retainers.

The company’s product logic is built in three layers:
a free cost estimator, a paid milestone tracker, and a premium expert consultation marketplace.
This is the core trust-ladder model: give value first, convert later.

## 2. The core problem Mkaan.AI solves

In India, first-time self-builders usually do not know the right cost of a house, the right stage-wise payment flow, the right quality checks, or the right approvals.
Contractors often control the information, and the homeowner becomes dependent on them.

That creates cost overruns, delays, confusion, and weak accountability.
Mkaan.AI’s thesis is that this is not just a construction problem; it is a trust and information problem.

* Users do not know what a fair estimate looks like.
* Users do not know which stage should unlock payment.
* Users do not know how to verify quality.
* Users do not know when to bring in professionals.

## 3. What the product actually does

The product is not just “software.” It is a guided construction intelligence system for homeowners.
It starts with a free estimate and then pushes the user toward deeper control and support.

| Layer | What it does | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Free estimate | User enters plot size, city, floors, and quality level to get a cost estimate. | Builds trust and helps the user understand the real budget. |
| Milestone tracker | Tracks construction stages, quality checkpoints, and payment release gates. | Helps users control contractor flow and avoid blind payments. |
| Expert marketplace | Lets users book architects, interior designers, and specialists for short sessions. | Makes professional guidance affordable and accessible. |

## 4. Mkaan.AI’s market position

Mkaan.AI is trying to become the homeowner trust layer for construction in India.

“I want to build a house, but I don’t know what’s fair, what’s correct, and whom to trust.”

In simple language: Mkaan.AI helps the user go from confusion to clarity, and then from clarity to control.

Phase 4 - Beginner Market Guide



# Phase 4: Beginner Market Guide

This phase explains the construction-tech market in simple words.
It is designed for someone who is new to the space and needs to understand the terms, categories, and product roles clearly.

## 1. What the market is about

The market is about helping people design, build, manage, monitor, procure, or understand construction.
Different companies solve different slices of that problem.

Some tools are for homeowners, some are for contractors, some are for developers, and some are for architects or engineers.
That is why one company is not enough to explain the whole market.

## 2. BIM

BIM means Building Information Modeling.
It is a process for creating and managing a digital model of a building that includes geometry, data, and coordination information.

BIM is not just a 3D model.
It is a shared information system that architects, engineers, contractors, and other stakeholders can use to coordinate work and reduce mistakes.

Easy way to remember BIM: it is a smart digital version of a building before it is built.

## 3. GTM

GTM means Go-To-Market.
It is the plan for how a company reaches customers and turns them into paying users.

In Mkaan.AI’s case, GTM likely means free estimate first, then tracker, then expert consults.
The product wins trust before it asks for payment.

## 4. CAC and LTV

CAC means Customer Acquisition Cost.
It is how much the company spends to get one customer.

LTV means Lifetime Value.
It is how much money that customer brings over time.

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| CAC | Cost to acquire one customer | Tells you whether marketing is efficient |
| LTV | Revenue earned from one customer over time | Tells you whether the customer is worth the acquisition cost |

A healthy business wants LTV to be much larger than CAC.
That means it earns more from a user than it spends to bring them in.

## 5. TAM, SAM, SOM

These are market-sizing terms.
They help explain how big the opportunity is and how much of it the company can realistically reach.

* **TAM:** Total Addressable Market. The full market size if you could serve everyone.
* **SAM:** Serviceable Available Market. The part of the market you can actually serve with your current model.
* **SOM:** Serviceable Obtainable Market. The share you can realistically win in a certain period.

## 6. BoQ

BoQ means Bill of Quantities.
It is an itemized list of work, materials, quantities, and rates for a construction project.

In simple terms, BoQ tells you what is being built, how much of each item is needed, and how much it should cost.
It is important because it helps reduce hidden charges and vague contractor pricing.

Easy way to remember BoQ: it is the item-by-item cost sheet for a construction project.

## 7. RERA

RERA is India’s real-estate regulation system.
It mostly applies to developer-led projects, not every self-built home.

That matters because many Mkaan.AI users are self-builders.
If a home is being built on a family plot or outside a developer project, the homeowner often has much less protection and structure.

## 8. QA/QC

QA means Quality Assurance.
QC means Quality Control.

In construction, these terms refer to making sure the work is being done correctly and checked properly.
Mkaan.AI’s tracker logic is related to QA/QC because it creates checkpoints and red flags.

## 9. Procurement

Procurement means buying materials and services for the project.
In construction, this includes cement, steel, pipes, fittings, tiles, and more.

BuildMitra-like companies focus on this layer.
They help with supplier access, rates, and ordering.

## 10. The market layers

The construction market is easier to understand if you split it into layers.
Mkaan.AI sits near the homeowner layer, but other companies solve adjacent layers.

| Layer | What it means | Example type |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Homeowner trust | Help the user understand and control the build | Mkaan.AI, Niwas-X |
| Workflow | Help teams manage tasks, admin, and schedules | EmendoAI |
| Quality | Help monitor progress and detect defects | Contineu |
| Procurement | Help buy materials and manage suppliers | BuildMitra |
| Execution / turnkey | Actually deliver the house construction | Thikedaar |

## 11. What each company caters to

* **Mkaan.AI:** First-time home builders who need cost clarity and control.
* **EmendoAI:** Contractors and construction businesses that need workflow automation.
* **Thikedaar:** Homeowners who want managed turnkey construction.
* **Contineu:** Developers and site teams who need visibility and defect detection.
* **BuildMitra:** Builders and contractors who need procurement support.
* **Niwas-X:** Homeowners who want AI advice for planning and costs.

## 12. What the product is really doing

Mkaan.AI is trying to sit between the user and the messy construction process.
It gives the user a starting estimate, a way to track progress, and a way to get expert help without traditional high fees.

In other words, it is helping the user move from uncertainty to clarity.

## 13. Why this guide matters

If you understand these terms and market layers, you can understand almost any discussion about the product.
You will know whether a feature belongs to the homeowner layer, the workflow layer, the quality layer, or the procurement layer.

The most important beginner idea is this: Mkaan.AI is not trying to be everything. It is trying to own the trust and decision layer for self-builders.

Mkaan.AI is the layer that helps ordinary homeowners understand, control, and trust the building process.






Phase 5 - Bottlenecks & First Actions



# Phase 5: Bottlenecks & First-Phase Actions

This phase explains what can go wrong, what bottlenecks can appear early, and what Mkaan.AI should do first.
It is meant to help you think like a product and strategy person, not just as a builder of features.

## 1. Why bottlenecks matter

Construction is a messy market. The product is not only competing with software; it is competing with habit, distrust, old vendor behavior, and offline relationships.

That means Mkaan.AI can fail even if the product is good, if the company does not solve trust, distribution, and user onboarding properly.

## 2. The biggest bottlenecks

| Bottleneck | What it means | Why it is dangerous |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Trust barrier | Users may not believe the estimate or the guidance. | If they do not trust the first output, they will not come back. |
| Data accuracy | Cost and rate data can become outdated or too generic. | Bad estimates damage credibility very fast. |
| Distribution | Users may not discover the product naturally. | Without reach, even a good product gets ignored. |
| Category confusion | People may think Mkaan.AI is just another construction software tool. | If the positioning is unclear, the product becomes generic. |
| Complexity | Too many features may confuse the user. | The user wants clarity, not a dashboard full of jargon. |
| Operational support | Expert network and support quality may be hard to maintain. | If expert quality is uneven, trust breaks. |

## 3. Product bottlenecks

The first bottleneck is the free estimate.
If users feel the estimate is too rough, too high, too low, or too generic, they may lose confidence immediately.

The second bottleneck is the milestone tracker.
If the tracker feels like extra work rather than a help, users may stop using it.

The third bottleneck is the expert marketplace.
If experts are not well-vetted, not responsive, or not relevant, the premium layer will not convert.

In this market, one bad experience can hurt the brand more than ten good marketing messages can fix.

## 4. Market bottlenecks

The market itself is hard because construction is still largely offline and relationship-driven.
Many users are used to asking a contractor or local engineer instead of a product.

That means Mkaan.AI must earn trust before it can scale.
It cannot rely on a pure “download and pay” model.

* Users may compare the app against their contractor’s advice.
* Users may think professional help is too expensive.
* Users may not understand the value of structured milestones at first.
* Users may only need the product once, so retention must be designed carefully.

## 5. Competitive bottlenecks

Public product pages show that EmendoAI is pushing strong workflow messaging, estimate generation, project management, and bookkeeping for contractors and renovation professionals.
Thikedaar has a strong turnkey execution story.
Contineu has site-visibility and defect-detection messaging.

This means Mkaan.AI cannot win only by saying “we also do estimates.”
It must be much clearer about why the homeowner-first trust layer is a better and more useful wedge.

| Competitor pressure | What it means for Mkaan.AI |
| --- | --- |
| EmendoAI | Competes on workflow depth and construction operations for businesses. |
| Thikedaar | Competes on turnkey trust and execution support. |
| Contineu | Competes on site visibility and quality intelligence. |
| BuildMitra | Competes on procurement and supplier access. |
| Niwas-X | Competes on homeowner-facing AI guidance and planning. |

## 6. First-phase actions

The first phase should not try to solve every construction problem.
It should focus on one sharp promise that is easy to explain and easy to test.

Suggested first-phase promise: “Get a realistic construction estimate and a trusted project roadmap in minutes.”

* Make the estimator very easy to use.
* Focus on city-specific cost logic.
* Keep the language simple and non-technical.
* Show the user what to do next after the estimate.
* Collect feedback from actual builders quickly.

## 7. Product-first action plan

| Phase | Action | Goal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase A | Launch a simple estimator with strong city-level messaging. | Get first trust and first users. |
| Phase B | Add a milestone checklist and payment-release logic. | Increase control and usefulness. |
| Phase C | Add expert consult booking. | Monetize trust. |
| Phase D | Improve data coverage and city expansion. | Scale with depth. |

## 8. What to avoid early

Do not start with too many modules.
The company should not try to be an ERP, a procurement marketplace, a site-monitoring company, and a consumer advisor at the same time.

Do not overuse jargon.
The user should not need to know BIM, BoQ, QA/QC, or workflow terms to benefit from the product.

The most dangerous early mistake is overbuilding before the product has a clear emotional hook.

## 9. What success looks like in the early phase

* Users get a clear estimate and trust it enough to act on it.
* Users keep coming back for milestone tracking.
